About

Everyone has heard horror stories about greek life hazing at universities. Many sororities across the country have gotten into trouble when word about their hazing done to their new members is discovered by their institutions. Many may believe that these stories are potentially made up, however, many young women have come forward and shared their experiences. At Montclair State University, a former sorority sister shared that the new members were forced to sit on washing machines as the current sisters of the sorority circled the parts of their body that jiggled. These new members were then told to lose the weight where the circles were on their body creating unrealistic body image standards for those young women. These types of hazing activities are not only present at that university, and with so many women across the country being affiliated with a sorority, many question how true the sisterhood of sororities really is. At many universities across the country, sororities are a large part of campus and student life. To many, sororities appear to provide many benefits to young women attending these universities. They can be a great way to make connections on campus, involve yourself within your community, and network through the millions of alumni spread throughout the country. There are undeniable benefits that sororities can provide for women. However, countless sororities have created a certain reputation for themselves on campuses, most of which are negative. There are many stigmas directed towards sororities and their members specifically. These include, but are not limited to bullying and stereotyping by other members, increased chances of getting sexually assaulted at one point in their college career, and the promotion of eating disorders and negative body image among young women. Young women being exposed to these social conditions and behaviors puts them in a fragile condition and heightens the risk of developing mental illness.

The Effects of Fraternity/Sorority Membership on College Experiences and Outcomes: A Portrait of Complexity

Click here to open this PDF in a new window


This is a study explaining the outcome of an experiment conducted to determine the self-esteem of both those who are accepted into a sorority and those who tried but failed to be let in. This experiment was conducted in a mid-atlantic area, where 591 students agreed to partake in this experiment. The subjects used the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale at two different times, once before their recruitment, and once after they had either quit, or finished their recruitment. From this study, experts concluded that girls who withdrew/weren’t accepted during recruitment had significantly lower self-esteem than those who were accepted into the sorority. This study suggests that the societal gravity and importance in getting accepted into a sorority is so high that it is causing girls that aren’t accepted to feel even worse about themselves. They also suggest that this study should be a great pathway for universities to create their own orientation that gives other opportunities for students other than greek life, and in that way hopefully raise the self esteem of these girls who aren’t accepted into the sorority by giving them other opportunities to participate in college life.

Image 4

This image shows a woman sitting with her head down in a quiet room, with what seems to be her shadow on the wall standing up. This picture is demonstrating a woman being depressed while picturing what she is envious of, a perfect body. This problem occurs a lot in females' lives, wanting to fix their physical appearance to satisfy themselves or others. Sororities make this problem even worse, as there are certain requirements in order to be accepted as a sister in a sorority. Women who are looking to join a sorority might not think they are “up to par” with their physical shape, they might try to fix it with unhealthy habits and ineffective ways, causing them to slump into a depression and think they will never be good enough to join a sorority.
